I've had a lot of time to think about :(failure(: over the past 15 months. How I failed myself, failed my colleagues, failed an organization, failed those sitting above or below me on the org chart, failed my friends, failed my family, failed my wife, and/or failed........ you get the picture.

Prior to August 2nd, 2024, I had spent the better part of 2 years preaching to two young gentlemen that the only way to improve as fast as we needed to was to fail twice as fast as that. We couldn't afford to speculate on what would or wouldn't work, we needed to move quickly and break everything. If we encountered anyone standing on the path forward screaming something wouldn't work, we needed to prove it. Fellas, until you show me it isn't, anything is possible.

And boy did we fail. Every now and again, catastrophically.

Nearly every day, I talked one person or another off a ledge. Often through their tears, sometimes through my own.

I don't subscribe to the theory that people don't want to change - at least not in full. Instead, we fear what trying to change will do to us or how it will make us look. Will I disappoint? Will I look stupid? Will I be wrong? What if I can't do it? What will any of this say about me?

I wasn't afforded the same luxury of doubt and fear as those around me were. At least outwardly. If I crack, then they crack, a dozen follow that, 50 follow them, and now everyone lets go of the rope. We didn't need everyone to understand every nuance. We didn't even really need everyone to agree with the direction we were headed. We did need everyone to hold onto the rope.

If I found you let go of the rope, it was my job to agree that, yes, we should have provided better rope holding gloves. It was my job to agree that everyone needs a break and that this is hard. It was my job to be there first to remind everyone where the rope is. It was my job to round up the last of the day's stragglers and sneak a rope back into their hands.

In the end, it was me that snapped. Catastrophically. Not the rope, not them, but me.

While I'm not going to lie and say that I don't fear failure or change, I do find that I process this fear differently than most around me. The status quo does not work for me. I need to explore the boundaries around me at all times. When I find the first boundary, it's on to the next. And the next after that. I'm more afraid of being stagnant, than of tripping over some arbritrary line. Especially a line that only exists because someone was simply afraid of crossing it.
